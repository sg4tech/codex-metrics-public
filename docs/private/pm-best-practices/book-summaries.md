## Strategy and positioning

### Good Strategy / Bad Strategy — Richard Rumelt (2011)

The sharpest practical book on what a strategy actually *is*. Rumelt's core claim: a real strategy has a three-part "kernel" — an **honest diagnosis** of the situation, a **guiding policy** for dealing with it, and a **coherent set of actions** that carry out the policy. Most corporate "strategies" are bad because they are lists of goals, aspirations, or motivational statements with none of the three. The book teaches how to recognize bad strategy in the wild (fluff, failure to face the challenge, mistaking goals for strategy, bad strategic objectives) and how to structure good one (leverage, proximate objectives, chain-link systems, design). Best used when someone asks you to critique a "strategy deck" that is actually a wish list.

### Playing to Win — A.G. Lafley and Roger Martin (2013)

Strategy as a cascade of five integrated choices: (1) winning aspiration, (2) where to play, (3) how to win, (4) what capabilities must be in place, (5) what management systems are required. The book is built around Lafley's time at P&G. Where Rumelt teaches diagnosis, Martin teaches choice-making — specifically the discipline that every "yes" implies "no"s, and that a real strategy is integrated (the five choices reinforce each other) rather than additive. Best used when shaping a plan from scratch rather than critiquing one.

### 7 Powers — Hamilton Helmer (2016)

A typology of durable competitive advantage, i.e., "powers" that produce differential returns against a determined, well-funded competitor. The seven: **scale economies, network economies, counter-positioning, switching costs, branding, cornered resource, process power**. Each is defined with a benefit (why it raises margins/volume) and a barrier (why competitors can't replicate). The book is short, academic in tone, and rewards careful reading — Helmer is explicit that if your business lacks one of the seven, it has no power and will converge to commodity margins. Best used to test whether a product's moat is real or imagined.

### Obviously Awesome — April Dunford (2019)

The only book that treats positioning as a teachable craft rather than a branding mystery. Five components: **competitive alternatives, unique attributes, value, best-fit customers, market category**. Dunford argues that positioning is usually the hidden cause of go-to-market failures — a product with good positioning sells itself, a product with bad positioning requires heroics. The book includes a 10-step repositioning exercise. Best used when messaging, sales decks, and growth efforts all feel harder than they should be.

### Sales Pitch — April Dunford (2023)

Companion to *Obviously Awesome*. Shows how to translate positioning into a sales narrative structured around the buyer's choice, not the seller's features. Core argument: customers decide *by comparing alternatives*, so a pitch must start from the customer's situation, name the set of alternatives, and explain why the category itself matters before showing the product. Best used for SaaS sales decks and founder pitches that currently lead with features.

### Crossing the Chasm — Geoffrey Moore (1991, updated 2014)

The technology adoption lifecycle — innovators, early adopters, early majority, late majority, laggards — and the "chasm" between early adopters (who buy on vision) and the early majority (who buy on references and conservatism). Moore's thesis: most tech startups die in the chasm because tactics that worked for early adopters (evangelism, custom builds) fail for pragmatists who want whole products with references. Solution: a beachhead strategy — dominate one pragmatist niche completely before expanding. Best used for B2B products past early adopters, stuck on repeatable sales.

### Competitive Strategy — Michael Porter (1980)

The original Five Forces framework (threat of new entrants, bargaining power of suppliers, bargaining power of buyers, threat of substitutes, industry rivalry) and the three generic strategies (cost leadership, differentiation, focus). Dense and academic, but foundational for any industry-level analysis. The argument that "stuck in the middle" (pursuing two generic strategies simultaneously) leads to underperformance remains contested but influential. Best used for industry analysis, not for early-stage startup strategy.

### Zero to One — Peter Thiel (2014)

Thesis: technology progress means going from zero to one (novel value) rather than one to n (incremental copying). Most memorable for the "seven questions every business must answer" (engineering, timing, monopoly, people, distribution, durability, secret), the claim that "competition is for losers" (you want monopoly, not commodity margins), and the argument that great companies find a "secret" — something true that few believe. Polarizing in tone, but the frameworks are operationally useful. Best used for first-principles thinking on what to build.

## Discovery

### Continuous Discovery Habits — Teresa Torres (2021)

The canonical modern book on how product teams stay close to customers. Introduces the **Opportunity Solution Tree** (outcome → opportunities → solutions → assumption tests), a practice of **weekly customer interviews** (1+ per week, conducted by the product trio), and an emphasis on **assumption mapping** before committing to solutions. Torres's argument is that discovery must be continuous, not a phase; that the trio (PM + design + engineer) must all participate; and that the tree prevents teams from jumping from research directly to solutions without considering alternatives. Best used to install a sustainable discovery practice in a team that currently does episodic "research sprints."

### What Customers Want — Tony Ulwick (2005) / Jobs to be Done (2016)

Outcome-Driven Innovation (ODI), the most formalized version of JTBD. Ulwick's argument: customers hire products to get "jobs" done, and jobs are best specified as desired outcomes measured on direction-of-improvement and importance. The ODI method: interview customers to extract ~100 outcome statements per job, rate them for importance and satisfaction, find the gaps (important + unsatisfied = opportunity). Rigorous, research-heavy, and best suited for established markets with quantifiable customer segments. Best used when you need defensible, quantitative prioritization of opportunity spaces.

### The Innovator's Dilemma — Clayton Christensen (1997)

Why great companies fail by doing everything right. Christensen's thesis: incumbents optimize for their best customers (sustaining innovation) and miss disruptive innovations that initially serve worse-off segments but improve over time, eventually displacing the incumbent. Introduces the distinction between sustaining and disruptive innovation, and shows through case studies (disk drives, steel, excavators) how disruption follows predictable patterns. Foundational reading — every PM should internalize the framework, even though applications in software are often misused. Best used to stress-test defensive strategies against lower-end entrants.

### Competing Against Luck — Clayton Christensen et al. (2016)

Christensen's book-length treatment of Jobs-to-be-Done theory. Core claim: customers don't buy products; they hire them to make progress in specific circumstances. Introduces the famous milkshake case study. More narrative than Ulwick's ODI and more accessible, less rigorous. Distinguishes "job" (the progress) from "solution" (the product) and shows how framing around jobs prevents feature-factory behavior. Best used as an accessible entry to JTBD thinking.

### Demand-Side Sales — Bob Moesta (2020)

The most practical book on JTBD interviewing technique. Moesta worked with Christensen and refined the "switch interview" — walk a recent buyer backward through the exact timeline of their decision to find the *forces of progress*: push (dissatisfaction with old), pull (attraction of new), anxiety (fear of change), habit (inertia). A switch happens when push + pull > anxiety + habit. The book reframes selling as "helping the customer make progress," not overcoming objections. Best used to learn how to actually conduct JTBD interviews, not just theorize about them.

### Interviewing Users — Steve Portigal (2013, 2nd ed. 2023)

The craft of interviewing, separated from any specific methodology. Covers question design, active listening, handling silences, managing group dynamics, and the skill of going from "the stated answer" to "the underlying reality." Short, practical, method-agnostic — applies equally well to JTBD, usability, and open-ended research. Best used by anyone who has ever received a flat or misleading interview transcript and wondered why.

### Just Enough Research — Erika Hall (2013, 2nd ed. 2019)

Antidote to "research theatre." Hall's argument: most teams either do no research or do bloated, ceremonial research that produces slide decks no one reads. Instead, do small, targeted research that directly answers decisions at hand. Covers organizational research, user research, competitive research, and the fundamental types of research questions. Short, opinionated, practical. Best used to install a research practice in a team with no research function, or to deflate a team with too much research bureaucracy.

### Practical Empathy / Mental Models — Indi Young

Young's work on deep listening sessions (not "interviews") that produce mental-model diagrams showing how different types of people think. More qualitative and more time-intensive than typical UX research. Best used when a team needs to differentiate between types of users with meaningfully different internal logic — common in complex B2B, healthcare, financial products.

### The Four Steps to the Epiphany — Steve Blank (2005)

The precursor to Lean Startup. Blank codifies "customer development" as a parallel track to product development: discover customers, validate customers, create customers, build the company. The book is dated in format (long, academic) but contains the original formulation of "get out of the building" and many patterns Ries later popularized. Best used for founders who want the full, slow, original version rather than the Lean Startup condensation.

## Lean and validation

### The Lean Startup — Eric Ries (2011)

The book that popularized Steve Blank's customer development, coined **MVP** (minimum viable product), **build-measure-learn** loop, **pivot**, **innovation accounting**, and **validated learning**. The core claim: in conditions of extreme uncertainty, the correct unit of progress is "validated learning" — evidence that updates your model of the customer — not features shipped. The framework has been widely misapplied ("MVP" used to justify shipping bad products; "pivot" used to justify aimless thrashing), but the underlying discipline of hypothesis-driven product work remains sound. Best used as a mental model, not as a methodology to be followed verbatim.

### Running Lean — Ash Maurya (2010, updated 2022)

Practical companion to Lean Startup. Introduces the **Lean Canvas** (a one-page business model adapted from the Business Model Canvas, with more emphasis on problem and unfair advantage). Walks through the process of moving from problem-solution fit to product-market fit to scale, with specific experiments and validation checkpoints at each stage. Best used as a step-by-step playbook when you want more structure than *The Lean Startup* provides.

### The Lean Product Playbook — Dan Olsen (2015)

Operationalizes Lean Startup into a concrete six-step **Product-Market Fit Pyramid**: target customer → underserved needs → value proposition → feature set → UX → PMF. Clear, actionable, less philosophical than Ries. Includes templates and case studies. Best used when you want a linear, repeatable process to follow from concept to PMF.

### Escaping the Build Trap — Melissa Perri (2018)

The clearest articulation of the **output vs outcome** distinction. Perri's thesis: many product organizations are stuck in the "build trap" — measuring themselves by features shipped rather than outcomes achieved. Prescribes a product organization structured around outcome ownership, proper PM training (versus project-manager-in-disguise), and a product strategy that cascades from vision to outcomes to team priorities. Best used when a team ships a lot, reports on it enthusiastically, and still misses the metrics that matter.

### The Startup Owner's Manual — Steve Blank & Bob Dorf (2012)

Massive, encyclopedic follow-up to *The Four Steps to the Epiphany*. Fuller and more current version of customer development. Dense. Best used as a reference rather than a cover-to-cover read.

## Shipping and operating

### Shape Up — Ryan Singer (2019, basecamp.com/shapeup, free)

Basecamp's antithesis to Scrum. Core ideas: **fixed time, variable scope** — set an appetite (time budget) and cut scope to fit, rather than estimating. **Shaped work** — problems are chewed to a specific level of definition before entering the bet cycle. **Betting table** — decision forum that chooses which shaped projects to commit to for the cycle. **6-week cycles + 2-week cooldowns** — enough time for real work, cooldowns for fixing, researching, resting. No backlog grooming, no daily standups, no story points. Polarizing but well-argued. Best used in teams with autonomy over their cadence and a taste for deep work over predictable throughput.

### Working Backwards — Colin Bryar and Bill Carr (2021)

Two Amazon insiders explain the operating system: **PR-FAQ** (write the press release and customer FAQ before building), **6-pager** (narrative memo read silently at start of meetings, replacing slides), **single-threaded leader** (one person fully dedicated to each initiative, not matrixed), **two-pizza team** (small autonomous teams), **bar raiser** (hiring quality check), **"disagree and commit"**. The book is the most accessible explanation of why Amazon ships what it ships at the scale it does. Best used to borrow operating rituals, not to copy Amazon wholesale.

### High Output Management — Andy Grove (1983)

The original operations management book for tech. Grove's **output as the product of activity times the output per activity**, the **managerial leverage** concept, **task-relevant maturity**, and the origin of **OKRs** (Grove's "Management by Objectives" later brought to Google by John Doerr and renamed). Still the sharpest single book on running an engineering organization. Best used as foundational reading for any manager, PM or otherwise.

### Getting Things Done — David Allen (2001)

Personal productivity methodology. The core: capture everything into a trusted inbox, clarify each item (actionable vs not, next action), organize by context, reflect weekly, engage with confidence. Dated in tone but the underlying discipline — especially "next action" thinking and the weekly review — remains powerful for anyone juggling many threads. Best used to build a personal operating system that doesn't leak commitments.

## Metrics and analytics

### Lean Analytics — Alistair Croll and Benjamin Yoskovitz (2013)

The practical companion to Lean Startup. Key framework: **One Metric That Matters (OMTM)** per stage of growth, because focus beats coverage in early stages. Defines business archetypes (SaaS, e-commerce, two-sided marketplace, user-generated content, free mobile app, media) and prescribes the most important metric for each at each stage (empathy → stickiness → virality → revenue → scale). Best used when a team has too many dashboards and not enough clarity on which number actually reflects progress.

### Web Analytics 2.0 — Avinash Kaushik (2009)

Foundational digital analytics text. Kaushik's argument: data without a business question is just noise; analysts exist to help the business make decisions, not to produce reports. Introduces concepts like the analytics trinity (behavior, outcomes, experience), conversion attribution thinking, and the discipline of always tying a metric to a decision. Best used as a mindset reset for teams drowning in analytics dashboards that drive no decisions.

## Growth

### Hacking Growth — Sean Ellis and Morgan Brown (2017)

Codifies the **growth experiment process**: ideate → prioritize → test → analyze, run as a weekly cadence. Ellis popularized "growth hacking" in 2010 and this book is the practitioner's handbook — how to structure growth teams, design experiments, prioritize with ICE/PIE scoring, and avoid common pitfalls. Strongly biased toward consumer/SaaS self-serve motions. Best used to install a repeatable experimentation practice rather than to invent a strategy.

### The Cold Start Problem — Andrew Chen (2021)

The best treatment of **network effects** and bootstrapping multi-sided products. Chen's central concept: the **atomic network** — the smallest stable network below which the product has no value. The book covers cold-start (getting to atomic), hitting the ceiling (mature network dynamics), and the death spiral (network collapse). Case studies from Uber, Tinder, Zoom, Slack, and others. Best used when building anything with network effects — and for knowing whether your product has network effects in the first place, since most that claim to do not.

## Pricing

### Monetizing Innovation — Madhavan Ramanujam and Georg Tacke (2016)

Argument: **price before you build**, not after. Ramanujam identifies four pricing failure modes — feature shocks (too many tiers/features), minivations (under-priced and under-valued), hidden gems (valuable unshipped features), undead (products kept alive past their economic expiry) — and prescribes willingness-to-pay research as a PM skill, not a finance function. The book is based on Simon-Kucher's consulting engagements and is unusually rich in case studies. Best used before any pricing decision larger than a tier tweak.

### Confessions of the Pricing Man — Hermann Simon (2015)

Simon founded Simon-Kucher, the largest pricing consultancy. The book is strategic rather than tactical — covers pricing psychology, value-based pricing, the limits of cost-plus, and the common mistakes (discounting as a reflex, pricing to match competitors, pricing by committee). More conceptual than Ramanujam's book. Best used as a mental model for pricing decisions rather than a step-by-step method.

## Team, career, and leadership

### Inspired — Marty Cagan (2008, 2nd ed. 2017)

Foundational PM text. Cagan's core model: the **product trio** (PM + design + engineering lead) works together on outcomes, and the PM's job is to discover a product that is valuable, usable, feasible, and viable. Covers PM hiring, the product discovery process, the difference between product teams and feature teams, and the characteristics of strong product cultures. The most widely read PM book in the industry. Best used as the starting point for anyone serious about the craft.

### Empowered — Marty Cagan and Chris Jones (2020)

Sequel to *Inspired*. Thesis: the best product companies give teams **problems to solve**, not roadmaps to execute — and the job of product leaders is to develop the judgment of PMs and engineers to the level where that delegation is safe. Covers coaching, staffing, vision, strategy, and team topology. Best used for product leaders building an org, not for individual PMs.

### Transformed — Marty Cagan (2024)

Third in the series. A playbook for companies transitioning from feature-team mode to empowered-team mode. More operational than *Empowered*, with explicit change-management patterns. Best used in large organizations undergoing a product-operating-model transition.

### The Hard Thing About Hard Things — Ben Horowitz (2014)

Memoir plus tactical advice from building Opsware through to a16z. The book's strength is its unflinching discussion of situations most leadership books avoid: demoting friends, laying off at scale, handling a CEO's psychology under pressure, navigating "wartime" vs "peacetime" CEO modes. Not a PM book per se, but essential for anyone who will ever have to make hard calls about people. Best used for context on what leadership actually requires.

### An Elegant Puzzle — Will Larson (2019)

Engineering-management systems. Larson approaches management as a systems-design problem: team sizing, organizational topology, how to get an engineering team unstuck, reorganizations, metrics. Readable, practical, and explicit about tradeoffs. Best used by engineering managers but highly PM-readable.

### Staff Engineer — Will Larson (2021)

The staff+ engineer career path, based on interviews with practitioners. Defines the staff engineer archetypes (tech lead, architect, solver, right hand) and the skills required. Mostly engineering-focused but contains patterns (writing engineering strategy, running design reviews, navigating reorganizations) directly relevant to PMs working with senior engineers.

### The Manager's Path — Camille Fournier (2017)

Canonical guide to the tech management career ladder, from mentor → tech lead → engineering manager → director → VP → CTO. Fournier is direct about what changes at each level and what doesn't transfer. Best used for anyone considering or currently on the management track.

### The Making of a Manager — Julie Zhuo (2019)

First-time-manager playbook, from Zhuo's experience at Facebook. Clearest voice on running 1:1s, giving feedback, and handling the emotional texture of the role. Best used for new managers and for PMs managing PMs for the first time.

### Managing Humans — Michael Lopp / "Rands" (2007, multiple editions)

Essays on engineering management. Loose, anecdotal, readable, with a distinct voice. Covers office politics, performance management, the weird dynamics of engineering culture. Best used as a companion, not a primary text.

### Trillion Dollar Coach — Eric Schmidt, Jonathan Rosenberg, Alan Eagle (2019)

Profile of Bill Campbell, the coach to Google, Apple, and others. The core practice: coaching as an operating discipline, not a soft skill. Campbell's standard — make everyone around you better — is the book's central claim. Best used for managers and leads who want a coaching posture rather than a command one.

## AI-native product and engineering

### Designing Machine Learning Systems — Chip Huyen (2022)

The most comprehensive book on productionizing ML systems pre-LLM era. Covers data pipelines, training-serving skew, feature stores, model deployment, monitoring, drift detection. Written from industry practice at Snorkel, Snap, NVIDIA, Stanford. Less focused on LLMs, but the operational patterns (observability, monitoring, data quality) translate directly. Best used as foundational reading for anyone productionizing any kind of ML model.

### AI Engineering — Chip Huyen (2024)

The LLM-era sequel. Covers foundation models, evaluation methodology, prompt engineering, RAG, fine-tuning, inference optimization, and AI product design. The closest thing to a current textbook for AI engineers and AI PMs. Chapters on evaluation and on dataset curation are particularly strong. Best used as the current canonical reference for building with LLMs.

## Systems thinking

### Thinking in Systems — Donella Meadows (2008, posthumous from 1993 manuscript)

The single best introduction to systems thinking. Meadows introduces stocks, flows, feedback loops, delays, and the concept of **leverage points** — twelve places to intervene in a system, ranked from least to most effective. The essay's ranking (paradigms at the top, parameter tweaks at the bottom) is a lifetime framework for choosing interventions. Accessible, short, rewarding repeated rereading. Best used as foundational reading for anyone who makes decisions about complex systems — which is every PM.

### The Goal — Eliyahu Goldratt (1984)

Business novel that popularized the **Theory of Constraints**. Core argument: every system has exactly one bottleneck at a time, and the only way to increase throughput is to identify and exploit that bottleneck. The **Five Focusing Steps** — identify → exploit → subordinate → elevate → repeat — remain one of the most operationally useful frameworks in management. The novel form makes it read quickly; the ideas stick because of the narrative. Best used whenever a team is tempted to optimize a non-constraint (which is most of the time).

### Out of the Crisis — W. Edwards Deming (1986) / The New Economics (1993)

Deming's work on quality as a system property. The **14 Points for Management**, the **System of Profound Knowledge** (appreciation for systems, theory of variation, theory of knowledge, psychology), and the foundational argument that most variation is common-cause (system) and cannot be fixed by exhorting individuals. Dense and occasionally dated, but the conceptual foundation underlying lean, Six Sigma, and modern quality practices. Best used to understand why blaming individuals for systemic problems makes things worse.

### Toyota Production System — Taiichi Ohno (1988)

Primary source for lean manufacturing from its originator. Introduces just-in-time, jidoka (automation with a human touch), kanban, the Five Whys, and the systematic elimination of muda (waste). Dense, terse, sometimes confusingly translated. More influential than readable, but worth sampling as a primary source. Best used for leadership who want to cite Ohno accurately rather than via the secondary literature.

### The Fifth Discipline — Peter Senge (1990)

Systems thinking applied to organizational learning. Senge's five disciplines: personal mastery, mental models, shared vision, team learning, and systems thinking. Most useful for the catalog of **system archetypes** — recurring patterns like *limits to growth*, *shifting the burden*, *tragedy of the commons*, *escalation*, *success to the successful*, *fixes that fail*. Dense and occasionally meandering, but the archetypes alone justify the read. Best used to recognize organizational patterns you've been stuck in.

### Antifragile — Nassim Taleb (2012)

Third in Taleb's Incerto. Thesis: fragile systems break from shocks, robust systems absorb them, antifragile systems *benefit* from them — and the right design goal is often antifragility, not robustness. The concepts of **convex vs concave exposure**, **optionality**, and **via negativa** (improvement by removal) are directly useful in product decisions. Taleb's prose style is polarizing. Best used as a mental model for risk and asymmetric bets.

### Skin in the Game — Nassim Taleb (2018)

Follow-up focused on asymmetry of incentives. Argument: those who make decisions must bear the downside of those decisions, or the decisions drift toward risk-free optionality at others' expense. Directly relevant to product decisions where the PM sets a strategy but doesn't own the outcome. Best used to stress-test whether a decision-maker is exposed to the failure mode they're approving.

### The Black Swan — Nassim Taleb (2007)

First of Taleb's better-known works. Argument: rare, high-impact events dominate outcomes in many domains, and our models (which assume normal distributions and extrapolated trends) systematically miss them. More philosophical than operational, but essential for calibrating confidence in forecasts. Best used as a corrective to overconfident quantitative plans.

### A Pattern Language — Christopher Alexander (1977)

Architectural theory that became foundational to software design patterns (Gang of Four, Martin Fowler) and to product design. 253 patterns from towns to rooms, each describing a problem, its context, and a solution form. The underlying philosophy — that good design emerges from composing small, proven patterns that "feel alive" — has influenced Ruby on Rails, React, and countless product and design systems. Best used as a source of design sensibility rather than as a direct reference.

## Essays and memos

### "Good Product Manager / Bad Product Manager" — Ben Horowitz (1997)

The single most-cited PM document in the industry. Two pages, written when Horowitz was at Netscape. The core technique is contrast: "Good product managers do X. Bad product managers do Y." Still the sharpest quick calibration of PM quality in the wild. Best used as a 5-minute taste check — if you disagree with most of it, calibrate yourself, not the memo.

### "Founder Mode" — Brian Chesky / Paul Graham essay (2024)

Chesky's controversial 2024 talk at a YC event, written up by Graham. Thesis: the "manager mode" that business literature (and Cagan's empowered teams) teaches is wrong for founders — founders should stay deep in the details, run skip-levels, and operate at a depth management theory says they shouldn't. Sparked a major debate in 2024 about the right operating model for founder-led companies. Best used as a counterweight when the empowered-teams orthodoxy becomes dogma.

### Paul Graham essays (paulgraham.com)

Essays on startups, makers vs managers, investing, and broader technology culture. Key reads: *Do Things That Don't Scale*, *Maker's Schedule, Manager's Schedule*, *How to Start a Startup*, *Startup = Growth*. Not a PM corpus per se, but formative for clarity-of-thought. Best used as taste calibration and as first principles for early-stage decisions.

### "Your AI Product Needs Evals" — Hamel Husain (2024)

Founding essay on eval-driven development for LLM products. Husain's argument: evals are to AI products what unit tests are to software — not optional, and the limiting factor on how fast you can iterate. Covers eval categories (reference-based, LLM-as-judge, human), golden datasets, and the operational cadence of an eval-driven team. Best used by anyone building with LLMs who is currently "just looking at outputs."

### "First Round Review" and "Lenny's Newsletter" (ongoing)

Publication-form resources rather than books. First Round Review is the highest-signal editorial aggregator of operator interviews in tech. Lenny Rachitsky's newsletter and podcast cover the modern canon across growth, product, and careers. Best used as current-state indexes — search for specific topics as they arise, not as cover-to-cover reading.
