# Strategy and Positioning

Where to play, how to win, and how to make the answer legible to the buyer.

## Canonical sources

- **Richard Rumelt** — *Good Strategy / Bad Strategy*. The kernel: diagnosis → guiding policy → coherent action. The sharpest practical book on what a strategy actually is (and isn't).
- **Roger Martin** (with A.G. Lafley) — *Playing to Win*. Strategy as a cascade of five integrated choices: winning aspiration, where to play, how to win, capabilities, management systems.
- **Hamilton Helmer** — *7 Powers*. Typology of durable competitive advantages: scale economies, network effects, counter-positioning, switching costs, branding, cornered resource, process power.
- **April Dunford** — *Obviously Awesome*, *Sales Pitch*. The only book that treats positioning as a teachable craft. Five components: competitive alternatives, unique attributes, value, best-fit customers, market category.
- **Geoffrey Moore** — *Crossing the Chasm*. Tech-adoption lifecycle; the chasm between early adopters and pragmatic mainstream.
- **Michael Porter** — *Competitive Strategy*. Five Forces; generic strategies (cost leadership, differentiation, focus).
- **Peter Thiel** — *Zero to One*. Monopoly as the condition of value; the seven questions (engineering, timing, monopoly, people, distribution, durability, secret).

## Core frameworks

- **Rumelt's kernel.** A real strategy has three parts: an **honest diagnosis** of the situation, a **guiding policy** for dealing with it, and a **coherent set of actions** to carry out the policy. Anything else is a list of goals or aspirations.
- **Playing-to-Win cascade.** Five questions, all answered together: (1) What is our winning aspiration? (2) Where will we play? (3) How will we win there? (4) What capabilities must be in place? (5) What management systems are required?
- **7 Powers.** A business has a "power" if it has a condition that creates differential returns against a determined competitor. Without one of the seven, there's no moat.
- **Crossing the Chasm.** Early adopters buy on vision; mainstream buyers buy on references. The chasm is the reference vacuum in between.
- **Positioning (Dunford's five).** What would customers use instead of you? What do you do uniquely well? Why does that matter? Who cares most? What category frames that value?

## Practical principles

- **Strategy = integrated choices, not aspirations.** "Be the best" is not a strategy; it's an aspiration.
- **If it doesn't specify what you won't do, it isn't a strategy.** Every "yes" implies "no's" — make them explicit.
- **Positioning comes before copy, messaging, and brand.** Positioning answers "what are you." The rest is translation.
- **One category, clearly.** If customers can't name your category in one phrase, positioning has failed.
- **Distribution beats product more often than PM folklore admits.** Thiel's seventh question: do you have a credible distribution plan?
