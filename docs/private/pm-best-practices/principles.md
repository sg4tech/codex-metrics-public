# Principles: Great vs Competent, Tensions, Anti-Patterns

Taste-level material. Not frameworks — judgments that distinguish a great PM from a merely competent one.

## What distinguishes great PMs

1. **Strong opinions, loosely held.** Forms hypotheses and updates them on evidence, rather than defending them. The update is visible.
2. **Problem ≫ solution discipline.** Will not discuss solutions until the problem statement survives adversarial scrutiny.
3. **Evidence-hierarchy awareness.** Knows the weight of anecdote vs qualitative signal vs quantitative data vs controlled experiment, and does not confuse them.
4. **Written clarity.** Can compress strategy into a one-pager or six-pager. Does not hide behind slides.
5. **Chooses what NOT to do.** Sharper than the ability to generate options. Every "yes" implies "no"s — they are made explicit.
6. **Systems thinking.** Sees feedback loops (adoption, retention, growth, content) and bottlenecks rather than linear feature lists.
7. **Operating cadence.** Weekly review, monthly strategy check-in, quarterly replanning — in practice, not on paper.
8. **Skin in the game on a metric.** Owns one number. Reports on it unprompted.
9. **Craft in one underlying discipline.** Deep in one of: data, design, engineering, research, sales. Pure process-PMs are fragile.
10. **Writing over talking.** Decisions are documented, not just proclaimed.

## Key tensions in the field

Tensions without settled answers. A thoughtful PM holds the tradeoff, rather than picking a side dogmatically.

- **Cagan (empowered teams) vs Chesky (founder mode).** How autonomous should product teams be? Cagan: empower. Chesky (2024): founder must stay deep in the details. Both have evidence; the right answer is context-dependent.
- **Agile/Scrum vs Shape Up.** How much structure vs how much air for shaping? Scrum optimizes predictable throughput; Shape Up optimizes deep problem-solving.
- **Quantitative vs qualitative discovery.** Torres/Moesta vs Amplitude-school. Rarely a real conflict; often a capacity/skill limitation of the team.
- **PLG vs sales-led.** Not a binary. Most durable B2B products run both: PLG for SMB and discovery, sales for enterprise expansion.
- **Single NSM vs balanced scorecard.** One focus metric clarifies decisions but narrows visibility. Scorecards give visibility but dilute accountability.
- **Roadmap vs now/next/later vs no roadmap.** Intercom (no roadmap), Basecamp (Shape Up / betting), Linear (now/next/later) all ship well. The shared trait is that commitments match the available certainty.
- **Outcomes vs outputs (Perri).** Is the correct answer to always prefer outcomes? In practice, some outputs are commitments (contracts, dependencies, deadlines) and need to be tracked as such.
- **Move fast vs get it right.** False binary if framed that way. Real question: what is reversible vs irreversible, and match the speed to that.

## Anti-patterns

- **Feature requests as roadmap items without problem statements.** Customer said it; it went on the roadmap. No one asked why.
- **Activity dressed as outcome metrics.** "Launched four features," "shipped X endpoints," "ran Y experiments." None of these is an outcome.
- **"Customer said X" = "customer needs X".** Customers describe solutions they imagine; PMs' job is to hear the problem underneath.
- **Framework as ritual.** RICE scoring on 40 tickets. OKRs cascaded to 9 levels. Scrum ceremonies run because the calendar says so.
- **Specs written, strategy absent.** Detailed specs for features whose existence has not been justified.
- **Pilots without success criteria.** Launching an experiment and deciding afterwards whether it "worked."
- **Optimizing a non-constraint.** Tuning a page that is not the bottleneck in the funnel. Polishing copy when the problem is positioning.
- **Symptom treatment.** Patching incidents without asking why they were possible.
- **Stakeholder alignment as the goal.** Alignment is a consequence of good strategy, not a substitute for one.
- **"Data-driven" as epistemic cover.** Data without a hypothesis is just history. The claim "the data says" usually means "I chose data that agreed with me."
- **Roadmap negotiation as product strategy.** The loudest stakeholders' priorities become the plan.
- **Confusing stocks and flows.** Trying to move a stock (users) by touching another stock (features shipped) rather than a flow (signup rate, churn rate).

## Reading calibration

If you read one thing as calibration: **Ben Horowitz — "Good Product Manager / Bad Product Manager"** (1997 Netscape memo). Two pages. Still the sharpest quick taste-check on PM quality.
